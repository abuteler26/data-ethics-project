Project Abstract

How Data Accessibility Shapes Retail vs. Institutional Investors

We study how differences in information access affect decision-making, perceived fairness, and outcomes during high-volatility events.

Research Question

How does unequal access to real-time, high-quality data affect retail vs. institutional investors’ decision-making and performance during volatility spikes, and what ethical concerns arise for fairness, responsibility, and transparency in financial markets?

Why Is This Question Important?

Test

Our Evidence

  • Trade X – Retail-like
    A modern survey experiment where UChicago students make hypothetical trading decisions under limited / delayed information, mimicking a retail setting with 15-minute quote delays and less data.
  • Trade Y – Institutional-like
    The same survey investors, but now with richer, real-time information, approximating institutional data access.
  • Historical Check
    A historical Robinhood order-flow dataset (2018–2020) that shows how retail investors actually behaved around episodes of extreme volatility.

Why This Project Matters

Why We Care. During volatility spikes, retail investors often trade on delayed quotes, fragmented news, or social media, while institutional investors rely on real-time order books, analytics, and execution algorithms. That information gap can create an uneven playing field where some traders systematically face worse prices, more risk, or stronger behavioral biases. Understanding this gap matters for fairness (who bears the risk), responsibility (how platforms design access to data), and transparency (whether investors understand the limits of what they see).

What We Build On. Prior research in behavioral finance shows that retail investors trade more pro-cyclically, chase momentum, and underperform after costs relative to institutions. Work on payment-for-order-flow and zero-commission trading raises questions about execution quality and hidden costs. However, fewer studies directly compare perceived fairness, confidence, and expectations across different information environments during high-volatility events.

What We Do. We ask UChicago students to make two hypothetical trades: a “retail-like” delayed-data setting (Trade X) and an “institutional-like” richer-data setting (Trade Y). We measure expected returns, confidence, allocation size, risk attitudes, and fairness perceptions. We then connect these patterns to a real Robinhood order-flow dataset to see whether the behaviors we observe in the lab line up with how retail traders actually behaved in 2018–2020 during volatility spikes.

Limitations & Next Steps. Our survey investors are students making hypothetical choices, not real-money trades, and our institutional benchmark is a richer-data treatment rather than true institutional access. Future work could extend this design to professional traders, incorporate actual financial incentives, and test alternative information regimes (e.g., fee-based “premium” data vs. equal-access public feeds).
