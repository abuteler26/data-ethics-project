\ Project Abstract\

How Data Accessibility Shapes Retail vs. Institutional Investors

\

\ We study how differences in information access affect decision-making,\ perceived fairness, and outcomes during high-volatility events.\

\ \

\ Our project combines two sources of evidence:\

\
    \
  • \ A modern survey experiment where UChicago students make\ hypothetical trading decisions under two information environments:\ a retail-like setting (Trade X) with delayed / limited data,\ and an institutional-like setting (Trade Y) with richer,\ real-time information.\
    • \
  • \ A historical Robinhood order-flow dataset that captures\ how actual retail investors behaved around extreme volatility between\ 2018\'962020.\
    • \
\ \

\ Ultimately, we ask:\ \ \'93How does unequal access to real-time, high-quality data affect\ retail vs. institutional investors\'92 decision-making and performance\ during volatility spikes, and what ethical concerns arise for fairness,\ responsibility, and transparency in financial markets?\'94\ \

\ \

\ The survey allows us to capture today\'92s beliefs, risk attitudes,\ and fairness perceptions among retail-like investors,\ while the Robinhood data provides an external check using\ real-world trading outcomes.\

\

How the Pieces Fit Together

\
    \
  • \ Survey Analysis (Trade X vs. Trade Y) \'96 shows how\ better data access changes expected returns, confidence,\ allocation sizes, and fairness perceptions when the same person\ behaves as a \'93retail\'94 vs. \'93institutional\'94 investor.\
    • \
  • \ Order-Flow Analysis \'96 uses Robinhood data to see\ whether real retail behavior during 2020 volatility episodes lines up\ with what our respondents say they would do.\
    • \
  • \ Scenario Excel Model \'96 lets users vary assumptions\ about information delays, risk tolerance, and allocation rules\ to see how small frictions in data access can compound into\ meaningful performance gaps.\
